| The chapter critiques Luick’s push‑chain explanation of the Great Vowel Shift, arguing that it rests on weak logical grounds. Although Luick correctly notes a south‑north split—southern dialects diphthongized both high vowels while northern varieties did not—the inference that the shift must be a push chain is unwarranted. The push‑chain argument (that /u/ diphthongized only where /o/ raised and thus pushed it) is circular and oversimplifies the complex vowel situation, especially since northern dialects had already developed long /o/ before the shift and exhibited lower pressure for diphthongization. Further logical problems include: (1) the back high vowel cannot explain changes in the front vowel space; (2) high‑vowel diphthongization can occur without a concurrent raising of the adjacent lower vowel, as shown by modern varieties; (3) front and back diphthongizations appear independent of each other and of any “pushing” vowel. Consequently, the current form of the push‑chain theory should be rejected, though this does not rule out a more general push‑chain scenario where other vowels might have initiated the shift. The author favors a drag‑chain interpretation for most dialects, citing uniformitarian evidence that many modern dialects diphthongize high vowels without raising lower ones, and noting that northern English and Scottish varieties can be seen as conservative rather than exceptional. Contact with southern English cannot fully explain the changes, as similar high‑vowel diphthongizations occurred in many unrelated languages under diverse conditions. Ultimately, English was never a monolithic system; different dialects likely followed different pathways, which lessens the urgency of solving the dialect and inception problems of the Great Vowel Shift. In early‑15th‑century London two speech systems co‑existed; the disappearance of –e removed the grammatical cue separating them, allowing the broader System II to dominate. Social groups such as the upward‑mobile “Mopsae” and the East‑Anglian “Easterners” spread these patterns after the 17th‑century civil wars, forming the basis of modern Received Pronunciation. | The passage explains that the Great Vowel Shift was triggered by the interaction of regional vowel systems and social change. East Anglia originally had a three‑height vowel inventory (/iː, uː, eː, oː, aː/). In the 15th century increased trade and migration brought this system into contact with the prestigious London system, where /aː/ was [aː], /eː/ was a French‑influenced [eː] and /iː/ already had diphthongal variants. Lacking the grammatical marker –e, East Anglian speakers substituted the nearest vowel from the London prestige, leading to raising and diphthongisation that later became the dominant 18th‑century prestige pronunciation. The “Mopsae,” descendants of the non‑prestige London System II, had by the early 17th century diphthongised /iː, uː/, raised /eː, oː/ and merged /aː/ with /eː/; their upward‑mobile speech spread after the mid‑17th‑century Civil Wars. The prestige of System I in the late 14th–early 15th centuries is attributed to rising middle‑class immigrants from the East and Central Midlands, whose weak social ties made them eager to adopt courtly speech. The combination of Middle English Open‑Syllable Lengthening, loss of final –e, and dialect contact in a rapidly changing urban society explains both the rapid onset of the Shift and its eventual cessation, since the loss of –e could not recur.
