A detailed study of the Great Vowel Shift shows its phonetic pattern of raising and diphthongising long vowels and attributes its trigger to a combination of extralinguistic developments (rapid urbanisation in southeast England, emergence of a class‑based prestige, increased social mobility after 1400) and intralinguistic changes (loss of final –e and phonemicisation of Middle English Open‑Syllable Lengthening). The shift was regionally varied: southern and mid‑land English underwent a full GVS, while northern English and Scots experienced a distinct “Northern Shift” linked to Scandinavian contact. In early‑15th‑century London two speech systems co‑existed; the disappearance of –e removed the grammatical cue separating them, allowing the broader System II to dominate. Social groups such as the upward‑mobile “Mopsae” and the East‑Anglian “Easterners” spread these patterns after the 17th‑century civil wars, forming the basis of modern Received Pronunciation. | The passage explains that the Great Vowel Shift was triggered by the interaction of regional vowel systems and social change. East Anglia originally had a three‑height vowel inventory (/iː, uː, eː, oː, aː/). In the 15th century increased trade and migration brought this system into contact with the prestigious London system, where /aː/ was [aː], /eː/ was a French‑influenced [eː] and /iː/ already had diphthongal variants. Lacking the grammatical marker –e, East Anglian speakers substituted the nearest vowel from the London prestige, leading to raising and diphthongisation that later became the dominant 18th‑century prestige pronunciation. The “Mopsae,” descendants of the non‑prestige London System II, had by the early 17th century diphthongised /iː, uː/, raised /eː, oː/ and merged /aː/ with /eː/; their upward‑mobile speech spread after the mid‑17th‑century Civil Wars. The prestige of System I in the late 14th–early 15th centuries is attributed to rising middle‑class immigrants from the East and Central Midlands, whose weak social ties made them eager to adopt courtly speech. The combination of Middle English Open‑Syllable Lengthening, loss of final –e, and dialect contact in a rapidly changing urban society explains both the rapid onset of the Shift and its eventual cessation, since the loss of –e could not recur.
